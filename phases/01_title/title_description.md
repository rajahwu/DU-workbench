The screen is dead gray. Not silver, not charcoal — the flat gray of stone that hasn't seen light in a long time. A gradient bleeds vertically, lighter at the center, darker at the edges, like a wall sweating condensation in a place that shouldn't be wet.

Then the paint stroke. A single horizontal swipe across the middle of the screen — thin, deliberate, like someone dragged their finger through wet ash. It thins as it moves left to right, tapering to almost nothing, the way a breath runs out. The gray beneath it shows through in uneven patches. This isn't decoration. This is a mark left behind. A tally. A warning.

The letters are already there. They've been there the whole time, hiding in the gradient — along the edges, pressed into the margins where the bars frame the viewport. You almost didn't see them. They sit in the architecture of the screen itself, woven into the lines the way names get carved into prison walls.

Then the color finds them.

It doesn't arrive all at once. It seeps. The letters along the top bar darken first, pulling away from gray into something that looks like dried blood seen through smoke. A deep blackened red that isn't warm — it's the red of something that burned a long time ago and never cooled all the way. It crawls down the side bars, touching each letter individually, like fingers pressing into wounds to check if they're still alive.

The screen holds this for one breath. Gray field. Ash stroke. Blackened red text bleeding at the edges. Silent.

Then the spark.

It ignites at the far left seam where the top bar meets the side — a pinpoint of deep dark gold, so compressed it looks almost black at its core. It sits there for a fraction of a second, gathering. Then it *moves*. Not smoothly. It zips — jagged, electric, like something escaping along a crack in a wall. Behind it, the seam it touches flares in a gradient that tells you what the spark is made of: black at the origin, bleeding into gold, gold bleeding into red, the red fading into the dark it already passed through. The trail sears and cools almost instantly, like a fuse burning in reverse.

The spark reaches the corner and turns. It runs the top seam now, and as it passes, each blackened red letter flares gold for an instant — lit from inside — then settles back darker than before. The spark carries a tip of radiant white, a single point of pure light riding the front edge of the gold, and it moves too fast to focus on. You don't see it. You feel it. It's the thing your eye chases but never catches.

It traces every seam. Top bar. Right side. Bottom. Left. A complete circuit. And the trail it leaves isn't just color — it's heat. The bars themselves look like they're remembering what it felt like to be forged.

When the spark completes the circuit and meets its own origin point, the screen brightens. Not gradually. The luminance *pulls* — like the whole display is inhaling. The gray field washes toward white. The ash stroke disappears. The letters glow gold, then white, then brighter than white, and the brightness keeps climbing past what the screen should be able to produce.

A bell chimes. One single tone — clear, high, with a resonance that sits in the chest. Not a notification sound. A consecration.

And the light implodes.

It doesn't fade. It doesn't spread. It collapses *inward* — every photon on the screen rushing toward the center point where the paint stroke was, pulling the white and the gold and the red and the gray all into a single shrinking point of impossible brightness. The edges of the screen go black first. Then the middle. The point compresses until it's smaller than the spark was, smaller than a pixel, and for one frame the entire screen is black except for that single white-gold dot.

Then it drops.
